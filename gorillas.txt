The Legacy of the Susa Family

The Susa Family is one of the most famous gorilla families in Volcanoes National Park, carrying a history filled with both triumph and tragedy. For decades, they have been at the heart of gorilla research and tourism, captivating visitors from around the world with their strength, resilience, and deep social bonds.

In 2002, the Susa Family endured one of the darkest moments in Rwanda’s conservation history when poachers brutally attacked them, killing two females and stealing a baby gorilla. The family never fully recovered from that trauma, but their story became a symbol of resilience. With time, healing, and strong leadership, Susa continued to grow, inspire, and represent the heart of Rwanda’s gorilla heritage.

Their fame is also tied to Dian Fossey, who closely studied the family for years, leaving a global legacy of gorilla conservation through her work at Karisoke Research Center.

	•	Isimbi Family – Known for its youthful energy and playful spirit, representing rebirth and renewal.
	•	Karisimbi Family – A majestic group that roams higher altitudes, carrying with it the legacy of the great Karisimbi volcano.

Each division carries the DNA of the legendary Susa lineage, ensuring that its story lives on through new generations.

The Pablo family’s story is one of strength, ambition, and legacy — a tale that begins in the heart of the Virunga Massif, high on the mist-covered slopes of Mount Karisimbi. Formed in the early 1990s, this iconic group traces its roots back to the legendary Susa family, one of the first mountain gorilla groups studied by Dian Fossey. Within Susa lived a powerful and charismatic silverback named Pablo, whose leadership, intelligence, and bold personality would change the course of gorilla history forever.

A Leader Is Born
Pablo’s rise to leadership was no accident. As he grew older and more dominant, his instinct to lead and protect pushed him toward independence. In the mid-1990s, he made a daring decision — to break away from the Susa family and create his own. With several loyal females by his side, Pablo formed a new group that would soon become one of the most successful and influential in Volcanoes National Park. This was the birth of the Pablo Family — a name that would echo across the mountains for decades to come.

Why “Pablo”?
The name Pablo was inspired by his confident, assertive nature and strong leadership style — much like a leader who commands authority and respect. Known for his calm but commanding temperament, Pablo was a protector and strategist. He led with strength yet rarely resorted to unnecessary aggression, a balance that made him deeply respected by his family and feared by rivals.

A Kingdom in the Clouds
Under Pablo’s disciplined leadership, the group thrived in the rich bamboo and hagenia forests above 3,500 m. Abundant food, safety, and a stable social structure allowed the family to grow rapidly. Within just a few years, the Pablo family reached a record-breaking 65 members, the largest gorilla group ever recorded in Volcanoes National Park. Visitors lucky enough to encounter them witnessed a breathtaking sight — multiple silverbacks standing guard, mothers nurturing their infants, and playful juveniles tumbling through the misty undergrowth.

A Legacy That Spread Across the Virungas
As younger males matured and sought independence, natural splits occurred — a normal part of gorilla society. From these divisions emerged many new families, each carrying Pablo’s genes, strength, and social wisdom into the future. Among them were Isabukuru, Gushimira, Dushishoze, Musirikare, Kureba, Mutobo, Iyambere, Urugwiro, Mafunzo, and more. Some, like Musirikare, originally broke away directly from Pablo’s group in the early 2000s, continuing his lineage and leadership legacy.

The Pablo Family Today
Though the original silverback Pablo has long passed, his legacy remains deeply woven into the forest’s fabric. Today, the Pablo family is still thriving under the leadership of a dominant male from his lineage — a testament to the strength of the dynasty he founded. The group remains one of the most impressive in the Virungas, carrying forward the traditions of power, discipline, and unity established by Pablo decades ago.

The Mountain Gorillas: A Legacy from 1967 to Today

“From Shadows of Extinction to a Symbol of Hope”

Origins of a Mission: Dian Fossey’s Journey Begins

In 1967, an American primatologist named Dian Fossey stepped into the heart of Central Africa with one unshakable mission: to study and protect one of the world’s most endangered primates — the mountain gorillas (Gorilla beringei beringei). Inspired by the pioneering work of paleoanthropologist Louis Leakey, who believed that studying great apes could unlock secrets of human evolution, Fossey left her career as an occupational therapist in the United States to follow a deeper calling.

Her first destination was Kabara, in the Virunga mountains of eastern Congo (now DRC), where she established a small research base in the montane forests. There, she began her first observations, often crawling on all fours to gain the trust of the gorillas she so deeply admired. Among her earliest collaborators were Alan and Joan Root, wildlife filmmakers who guided her into the forest, and Dr. George Schaller, one of the first scientists to conduct systematic field research on gorillas.

Fleeing Conflict and Finding a Home in Rwanda

Political instability in Congo in 1967 forced Fossey to abandon Kabara. Determined to continue her work, she crossed the border into Rwanda, choosing a mist-shrouded slope between Mount Bisoke and Mount Karisimbi — a location rich in gorilla populations and remote enough to allow close, long-term observation. Here she founded the now-legendary Karisoke Research Center on September 24, 1967.

The name Karisoke itself was a combination of Karisimbi and Bisoke, the two volcanic giants that cradle the forest. From this base, Fossey and a small team of field assistants — including notable researchers like Kelly Stewart, Alexander Harcourt, Craig Stanford, and Bill Weber — transformed gorilla research from basic observation into a lifetime scientific pursuit.

The First Gorilla Families: Names, Stories, and Bonds

At Karisoke, Dian Fossey encountered and habituated her first gorilla families. Among them were:
	•	Group 4 and Group 5, later known as Shinda’s lineage
	•	Group 8, which evolved into Beetsme’s family
	•	Group 11, which eventually led to the iconic Pablo group

She gave many gorillas individual names — Digit, Peanuts, Macho, and Uncle Bert — recognizing their unique personalities and social dynamics. These names humanized the apes and helped the public form emotional connections with them, a crucial step in building global support for their protection.

A Battle Against Poaching

Fossey’s time in the Virungas was marked not only by scientific discovery but by fierce conservation battles. Poaching was rampant in the 1960s and 70s, driven by the illegal pet trade and demand for trophies. The most devastating moment came in 1977, when her beloved silverback Digit was killed by poachers. This tragedy led Fossey to launch The Digit Fund (now the Dian Fossey Gorilla Fund), channeling global attention and resources into anti-poaching patrols and habitat protection.

Gorilla Population Trends: From Crisis to Recovery

When Fossey began her work in 1967, fewer than 250 mountain gorillas were estimated to exist. By 1980, after years of poaching, habitat destruction, and disease, the population hovered around 250–270 individuals.
But conservation efforts, fueled by her legacy, began to turn the tide:

Year	Estimated Population	Milestone
1967	~250	Fossey begins research
1980	~270	First census after intensive study
1989	~320	Anti-poaching patrols expand
2003	~380	Community tourism programs launched
2010	~480	Improved protection and research
2018	~604	IUCN reclassifies mountain gorillas from “Critically Endangered” to “Endangered”
2024	~1,063	Latest census across Virunga Massif and Bwindi

Lineages and Legends: The Families She Studied

From Fossey’s early research came dynasties that still exist today:
	•	Beetsme Lineage – Produced iconic groups such as Kuryama, Bwenge, Sigasira, and Titus.
	•	Pablo Lineage – One of the largest gorilla families ever recorded, leading to Isabukuru, Musirikare, Mutobo, Iyambere, and others.
	•	Shinda Lineage – Gave rise to Ntambara, Urugamba, and Ugenda groups.

These family names — born in the forests of Karisoke — remain the foundation of gorilla monitoring today.

Today’s Gorilla Families in the Virunga Massif

Over five decades later, more than 30 habituated gorilla families roam the Virunga mountains, including:
	•	Susa, Karisimbi, Amahoro, Umubano, Sabinyo, Agashya, Hirwa, Pablo, Isabukuru, Muhoza, Kuryama, Ntambara, Urugwiro, Mafunzo, and more.

Each family represents generations of protection, research, and community collaboration that began with Fossey’s work.

Kwita Izina: The Power of Names

Since 2005, Rwanda’s annual Kwita Izina gorilla naming ceremony has celebrated newborn gorillas and raised global awareness. Inspired by traditional baby-naming rituals, this event connects people to gorillas as individuals — strengthening conservation bonds.
	•	More than 400 baby gorillas have been officially named since the ceremony began.
	•	The event highlights the importance of community involvement, as many namers include rangers, local leaders, scientists, and schoolchildren.

Future of the Forest: Expansion and Hope

The future of mountain gorillas is closely tied to habitat protection. Efforts are underway to expand Volcanoes National Park by nearly 37 square kilometers, restoring forest corridors and increasing feeding grounds for growing populations.

Today, about 20–25 gorilla births are recorded each year across the Virunga Massif — a testament to decades of collaborative conservation.

Communities at the Heart of Conservation

The survival of mountain gorillas is inseparable from the well-being of the people who live alongside them. Community-based tourism initiatives now ensure that a share of tourism revenue is invested in schools, health care, water systems, and livelihood programs. Local guides, trackers, and porters — once hunters — are now guardians of the gorillas’ future.

From the moment she knelt in the damp forest of Kabara to the day she was laid to rest among the gorillas she loved, Dian Fossey changed the course of conservation history. Today, more than 1,000 mountain gorillas thrive because of her courage, science, and devotion — a living legacy that continues to inspire the world.

The Origins of the Mutobo Family

In the dense, mist-covered forests of Volcanoes National Park, a story of ambition, rivalry, and resilience gave rise to one of the most remarkable gorilla families — the Mutobo Family. This group’s journey is a tale of power, perseverance, and the unbreakable instinct to lead, marking it as a living legend among the mountain gorillas of the Virunga Massif.

From Pablo Lineage to Independence

The roots of the Mutobo family trace back to the iconic Pablo group, one of the most studied and influential gorilla families in the region. Born into this lineage, Mutobo was destined for greatness. As a young and determined silverback, he grew under the shadow of powerful leaders, learning the delicate balance of leadership, protection, and strategy. Yet, his ambition was far greater than to remain a subordinate.

Driven by an instinctive need to carve out his destiny, Mutobo chose the path of independence. Around 2017, he made the bold decision to separate from his natal group, setting out into the forest with a vision of building a dynasty of his own.

A Silverback’s Rise: The Birth of a New Family

Mutobo’s journey to leadership was not without challenges. Soon after leaving the Pablo group, he encountered his brother Urugwiro, and the clash between the two was fierce and inevitable. Mutobo’s strength and determination prevailed — and with that victory came four females who chose to follow him.

But the turning point in his story came after the death of another prominent silverback, Mafunzo. Seizing the opportunity, Mutobo integrated Mafunzo’s females into his growing group. The Legacy of the Kwitonda Family

The Kwitonda Family holds a special place in the story of the Virunga Massif. Once led by the gentle giant, Silverback Kwitonda, this family embodied patience, wisdom, and quiet strength. Unlike other dominant males, Kwitonda was known for his calm nature, a leader who chose protection over aggression and guidance over force.

Arriving from the Democratic Republic of Congo in 2005, Kwitonda and his group of 16 members settled in Rwanda’s Volcanoes National Park, where their journey became a symbol of resilience and new beginnings. Over the years, the family grew and thrived under Kwitonda’s watchful care, expanding to more than 23 members. Researchers, rangers, and visitors alike were deeply moved by the family’s bond and the unique aura of their leader.

But just as the forest evolves, so too did the Kwitonda lineage. After the passing of Kwitonda, the family divided into new groups, each carrying forward his spirit in their own way:
	•	Kwisanga Family – Named for togetherness, this group reflects the unity and strength that Kwitonda instilled in his offspring.
	•	Lisanga Family – A symbol of new chapters and resilience, Lisanga represents the courage to forge ahead.
	•	Urwego Family – Meaning “pathway,” this family reflects a continuation of Kwitonda’s journey, reminding us that every ending is the beginning of another story.

The Legacy of the Susa Family

The Susa Family is one of the most famous gorilla families in Volcanoes National Park, carrying a history filled with both triumph and tragedy. For decades, they have been at the heart of gorilla research and tourism, captivating visitors from around the world with their strength, resilience, and deep social bonds.

In 2002, the Susa Family endured one of the darkest moments in Rwanda’s conservation history when poachers brutally attacked them, killing two females and stealing a baby gorilla. The family never fully recovered from that trauma, but their story became a symbol of resilience. With time, healing, and strong leadership, Susa continued to grow, inspire, and represent the heart of Rwanda’s gorilla heritage.

Their fame is also tied to Dian Fossey, who closely studied the family for years, leaving a global legacy of gorilla conservation through her work at Karisoke Research Center.

⸻

A New Chapter: The Divisions of Susa

Like many gorilla families, Susa eventually grew so large that it naturally divided into smaller groups, each forging its own identity on the forested slopes of the Virunga Massif:
	•	Igisha Family – A strong group led by Silverback Igisha, embodying authority and determination.
	•	Isimbi Family – Known for its youthful energy and playful spirit, representing rebirth and renewal.
	•	Karisimbi Family – A majestic group that roams higher altitudes, carrying with it the legacy of the great Karisimbi volcano.

Each division carries the DNA of the legendary Susa lineage, ensuring that its story lives on through new generations.
